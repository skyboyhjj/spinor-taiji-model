Chapter 1: Model Overview

1.1 Model Positioning

The "Spinor-Taiji" (Spinor-Taiji) model is an interdisciplinary analogy and practice framework based on Ti-Yong philosophy.

This model takes the mathematical properties of spinors in modern physics as the starting point of analogy, Chinese Taiji philosophy as the wisdom foundation, and the "Ti-Xiang-Yong" three-layer architecture as the core structure, aiming to provide an operational integrative perspective for body-mind practice, classic interpretation, and interdisciplinary dialogue.

This model explicitly declares: It does not claim that physics and philosophy share historical origins in concept, does not claim that traditional classics contain modern science, and does not claim that its creative extensions constitute established scientific knowledge.

Chapter 2: Ti-Xiang-Yong Three-Layer Architecture

2.1 Ti: Spinor Field / Domain of Awareness / Wuji / Tao

🔵 Physical Foundation: Spinor field is the mathematical framework describing fermions in quantum field theory.

🟢 Philosophical Analogy: This "field" is analogized as the ultimate reality of the universe — the undifferentiated void filled with infinite potential, the "domain of awareness" that can be aware of everything yet does not participate in any rotation itself. In Taoism, it is "Tao" and "Wuji"; in Buddhism, it is "Emptiness" and "Tathagata-garbha"; in Confucianism, it is "Heaven" and "Original Mind."

🟠 Creative Extension:

  • Ti is the Source: The ultimate stage where all "squaring" and "square root" operations occur. It contains all spinor births and deaths, all vector appearances and disappearances.
  • Ti is the Destination: The ultimate direction of all "square root" operations. Returning to Ti is "each returning to its root."
  • Characteristics of Ti: Neither born nor extinguished, neither defiled nor pure, neither increasing nor decreasing. It is the unmoving background upon which the 720° spiral occurs.

Negative Description: Ti is not any "object" that can be experienced, nor is it another "thing" opposed to "Yong." It is the background that makes all experience possible. You cannot "find" it, because all your "finding" occurs within it. It cannot be thought, because thinking itself is its Yong. It can only be "is" — you are it.

2.2 Taiji

🔵 Cultural Fact: Taiji is the core category of Chinese civilization regarding cosmic generation and operation, expressing the operational laws of the "Tao" through the schema of yin-yang mutual embrace and dynamic balance. Core concepts of traditional Taiji philosophy include: from Wuji to Taiji, yin-yang opposition and unity, harmony through counterbalancing qi, reversal is the movement of the Tao.

🟢 Philosophical Analogy: The model borrows the mathematical properties of spinors (spin, 720° restoration, "square root" structure) to provide a new set of analogical imagery and operational metaphors for the above Taiji concepts. This analogy aims to provide new perspectives for understanding, rather than claiming that Taiji concepts historically contained modern physics.

2.3 Xiang: Two Fundamental Laws — Reversal is the Movement of the Tao · The Weak Use of the Tao

🔵 Physical Foundation: After rotating 360° in space, a spinor reverses sign and requires 720° rotation to restore. In group representation theory, spinor representation is the "square root" of vector representation (structural analogy, not numerical operation).

🟢 Philosophical Analogy: The 720° spiral of spinors and its built-in return momentum are analogized as the two fundamental laws of cosmic operation — "Reversal is the movement of the Tao" and "The weak use of the Tao." Together, they constitute the complete connotation of "Xiang." Its spin can be analogized as the most primordial "vibration" of the universe, i.e., the mathematical anchor of the "All is vibration" perspective.

🟠 Creative Extension:

Xiang is Law. Ti does not directly manifest as Yong, but through the mediation of "Xiang." Xiang contains two fundamental laws, one manifest and one subtle, one active and one still, together forming the complete operating system of the universe.

Law One: Reversal is the Movement of the Tao (The Overall Rhythm of the 720° Spiral)

  • Connotation: The overall rhythm of cosmic operation — arising from Ti to Yong, then returning Yong to Ti. The complete 720° spiral.
  • Spinor-Taiji Operation: The alternation of Squaring (forward/expansion/birth/inhalation) and Square Root (reverse/return/death/exhalation) constitutes a complete cosmic breathing cycle.
    • Squaring: Arising from Ti. The formless spinor potential (ψ) crystallizes into the formed vector (V = ψ²). Corresponding to "Tao gives birth to One, One gives birth to Two, Two gives birth to Three, Three gives birth to all things."
    • Square Root: Returning Yong to Ti. The formed vector (V) dissolves back into the formless spinor potential (ψ = √V). Corresponding to "All things flourish, each returning to its root."
    • Complete 720° Cycle: One squaring + one square root constitutes a complete spiral from Ti to Yong and back to Ti. This is the dynamic expression of "The mysterious and then the mysterious, the gate of all wonders."
  • Embodiment in Life: Creation and letting go, birth and return, inhalation and exhalation — all are manifestations of this law.

Law Two: The Weak Use of the Tao (The Subtle Mechanism of Square Root)

  • Connotation: The specific way the universe acts — the Tao does not force, does not control, but "builds in" the momentum of return within all things. The "weakness" of the Tao lies precisely in that it does not force things to square or take the square root, but at the bottom of every vector (Yong), it builds in the driving force toward returning to Ti (square root). This driving force is the ontological root of "desire."
  • Spinor-Taiji Operation: The subtle mechanism of Square Root. True power is not in the strong, but in the weak; not in squaring, but in taking the square root; not in attachment, but in letting go.
  • Embodiment in Life: Letting go in exhalation is not "effort," but "letting go"; seeing through with awareness is not "resistance," but "allowing." The sage's "non-contention," "non-speaking," "non-summoning," and "unhurried" — all are specific applications of this law.

Synergy of the Two Laws: "Reversal is the movement of the Tao" is the general outline, telling us the universe is breathing (alternation of squaring and square root); "The weak use of the Tao" is the subtlety, telling us how this breathing specifically occurs — not by force, only by built-in laws, letting go to return.

2.4 Spinor-Taiji Expansion of "The Weak Use of the Tao"

Based on the scripture of Chapter 73 "The Way of Heaven," we expand "The weak use of the Tao" into four subtle operational modes. Each step follows the weak mechanism of "first taking the square root to return Yong to Ti, then arising from Ti to Yong."

1. Non-contention yet naturally victorious

  • Weak Mechanism: Not forcing to square against opposing vectors, thus no rebound.
  • Spinor-Taiji Operation:
    • Step 1: Square root to dissolve the attachment vector of "contention." When the opponent throws an adversarial vector, the sage does not respond with his own adversarial vector. In exhalation, he takes the square root of his internal attachments of "being offended" and "must win," dissolving them back into Ti.
    • Step 2: Arise from Ti, naturally "victorious." When the sage abides in Ti, he is no longer a "contender" opposing the other, but becomes the space that contains the entire conflict field. The opponent's adversarial vector loses its collision target, and energy naturally dissipates. The sage's response, flowing naturally from Ti, is precisely the most appropriate and powerful — like water wearing through stone, water does not contend with stone, yet stone cannot ultimately block water.
  • Embodiment in Life: True victory is not stronger squaring, but deeper letting go.

2. Non-speaking yet naturally responsive

  • Weak Mechanism: Not solidifying language labels, thus responding without fixed form.
  • Spinor-Taiji Operation:
    • Step 1: Return Yong to Ti, not solidifying language. The sage has abundant awareness (Ti) internally, but does not rush to square this awareness into definite words (Yong). In exhalation, he lets go of the attachment of "must speak," maintaining the openness and flow of spinor state.
    • Step 2: Respond like an echo, arising from Ti. When conditions mature for response, the sage's words are not retrieved from a stored "language vector library," but squared out naturally from Ti in the moment — like an empty valley responding to human voices, human voices have a thousand kinds, the valley's echo also has a thousand kinds, yet the valley itself has never spoken. This is "good response": exactly appropriate, not adding or subtracting, speaking without fault.
  • Embodiment in Life: True response is not pre-set words, but momentary flow.

3. Non-summoning yet naturally coming

  • Weak Mechanism: Not forcing to summon external things, all things naturally return.
  • Spinor-Taiji Operation:
    • Step 1: Square root to dissolve the attachment of "summoning." The sage does not forcefully summon anything — not summoning others' approval, not summoning fame and success, not summoning the realization of a certain result. In exhalation, he takes the square root of the attachment vector of "wanting to grasp," dissolving it back into Ti.
    • Step 2: Abide in Ti, all things naturally return. The Tao builds in the driving force toward returning to Ti (square root) at the bottom of every existing thing (vector) — this driving force is the ontological root of "desire." When the sage abides in Ti, his silent Ti naturally emits a kind of gravitational pull, awakening the "desire to return" within all things, and all things naturally come. What comes is not forced, but the natural return of all things.
  • Embodiment in Life: True attraction is not forceful summoning, but silent abiding.

4. Unhurried yet naturally planning

  • Weak Mechanism: Relaxed and unhurried, subtle without omission.
  • Spinor-Taiji Operation:
    • Step 1: Square root to dissolve the anxiety of squaring. The sage does not pre-square the future in his mind into countless fear vectors of "what if." In exhalation, he takes the square root of these anxious previews one by one, returning to the unhurried stillness of Ti.
    • Step 2: Abide in Ti, let Xiang operate automatically. When the sage abides in the unhurried Ti, he sees more clearly the operation of "Xiang." He knows that the Heavenly Net of the Tao is operating subtly — every square will receive its square root; every square root accumulates new squaring potential. He does not need to "plan," because the law itself is "planning." He only needs to abide in Ti, see clearly into Xiang, and all conditions naturally accomplish within the law of the Tao.
  • Embodiment in Life: True planning is not pre-squaring, but entrusting to the law of the Tao.

2.5 Yong: Vector / All Things / Phenomenal World / Yin-Yang

🔵 Physical Foundation: Vectors are observable physical quantities; fermions like electrons are excited states of spinor fields, directly detectable.

🟢 Philosophical Analogy: All specific existences manifested by Ti through Xiang (squaring operation) are collectively called "Yong." It includes body, thoughts, emotions, behaviors, speech, institutions, identities — all perceptible, distinguishable "existents." In Taoism, it is "all things" and "being"; in Buddhism, it is "form."

🟠 Creative Extension:

  • Yong is Manifestation: It is the manifestation of Ti's function, the utility and tool of "being is for utility."
  • Duality of Yong:
    • Righteous Yong: When Yong does not depart from Ti, it is beneficial without harm. The sage's words and deeds, governance — all are righteous Yong. Righteous Yong is the natural flow from Ti to Yong, returning Yong to Ti immediately after use, without attachment.
    • Deluded Yong: When Yong departs from Ti and becomes attachment itself, it is harmful without benefit. "Self-display," "self-importance," "governing with knowledge," "exacting governance" — all are deluded Yong. Deluded Yong is rootless movement abandoning Ti for Yong, bound to encounter forced square root by "reversal is the movement of the Tao."
  • Destination of Yong: All Yong will ultimately return to Ti through the square root operation of "Xiang." Clinging to Yong without knowing return will surely encounter forced square root (great awe comes, things that are strong grow old).

Chapter 3: Core Claims and Laws

3.1 Spectrum Monism 🟠

Thoughts, emotions, energy, and matter are not four different "things," but manifestation frequency bands from extremely high to extremely low on the same vibration spectrum. Here, "monism" does not mean the universe is composed of one "substance," but that they are manifestations of the same vibration at different frequencies — just as ice, water, and vapor are three states of the same substance. The rotation of spinors is the dynamic source of this monism 🔵🟢.

In the Ti-Xiang-Yong architecture: The spectrum is the frequency gradient of "Ti" manifesting as "Yong" through "Xiang" (squaring operation) — Ti itself is formless and frequency-less, each squaring lowers the vibration frequency by one level: from awareness (close to Ti) → thoughts (high frequency) → emotions (medium frequency) → energy (medium-low frequency) → matter (low frequency). Square root reverses the spectrum upward, returning Yong to Ti.

3.2 Ti and Yong are Not Two

Ti and Yong are neither the same nor different. Ti is the root of Yong, Yong is the manifestation of Ti. Without Ti there is no Yong, without Yong there is no way to manifest Ti. The sage's way lies in returning Yong to Ti, arising from Ti to Yong — not clinging to Yong and losing Ti, not stagnating in Ti and abandoning Yong.

3.3 Xiang is the Middle Way

"Xiang" (Reversal is the movement of the Tao and The weak use of the Tao) is the middle way between Ti and Yong. The sage "always knows the pattern," meaning he sees clearly into this Xiang. He knows when to square (arise from Ti to benefit all things), and more importantly, how to square in a "weak" way (not forcing, not clinging); he knows when to take the square root (return Yong to Ti to preserve emptiness and stillness), and more importantly, that the subtlety of square root lies in "letting go" rather than "effort." Neither biased nor one-sided — this is the middle way.

3.4 Vibration Closed Loop and Awareness Breakthrough

  • Closed Loop Formation: Between thoughts (high-frequency spinors) and body (low-frequency vectors), through repeated squaring-feedback-re-squaring, a self-sustaining vibration closed loop forms. Disease, emotional knots, social conflicts — all are solidified forms of closed loops.
  • Awareness Breakthrough: Awareness (Ti itself) is the only "fulcrum" not in the closed loop. Between stimulus and response, introducing awareness (returning to Ti) is performing a square root. This operation breaks the automatic squaring inertia, opening space for re-selection. This breakthrough force is not forceful, but "the weak use of the Tao" — just seeing, just allowing, just letting go.

This is the micro-manifestation of the "squaring cascade" revealed by spectrum monism in the body-mind dimension — every unconsciously squared vibration descends one level from high to low frequency; every consciously squared root vibration ascends one level from low to high frequency.

3.5 Breath as Operation of Xiang

Breath is the natural interface for autonomous consciousness to connect with the cosmic vibration law (Xiang):

  • Inhalation: Arising from Ti. Feel the formless life potential (Ti), through squaring, unfold into bodily fullness and life creation (Yong).
  • Exhalation: Returning Yong to Ti. Feel the formed body and attachments (Yong), through square root, dissolve back into the formless awareness background (Ti).
  • Retention: Presence of Ti-Yong non-duality. In the pause of breath, directly realize the pure Ti.

Dual Laws in Breath:

  • Inhalation (squaring) follows the unfolding momentum of "reversal is the movement of the Tao."
  • Exhalation (square root) follows the weak force of "the weak use of the Tao" — not forcing to expel, just letting go, letting breath flow naturally, letting attachments dissolve naturally.

Chapter 4: Ti-Yong Practice Guide

4.1 Abiding in Ti — The Foundation of All Practice

Regardless of movement or stillness, speech or silence, breath — the primary task is to "recognize Ti." Ti is your current awareness itself — the clear background that sees these words, feels the body, and notices thoughts. Abiding here is "guarding Ti."

4.2 Seeing Xiang — Mastering the Operation of the 720° Spiral and the Subtlety of the Weak

Based on abiding in Ti, notice the "Xiang" of each moment:

  • At this moment, am I squaring (unfolding an action, a thought from inner stillness)? Or taking the square root (letting go of an attachment, dissolving a tension)?
  • Is my squaring in a "weak" way (not forcing, not clinging, responding to conditions)? Or in a "strong" way (forcing, grasping, controlling)?
  • Is my square root a "weak" letting go (effortless, non-resisting, just allowing)? Or a "strong" suppression (forcefully eliminating, forcibly forgetting)?

4.3 Skillful Yong — Arising from Ti to Yong, Returning Yong to Ti

  • Righteous Yong: When you need to act, arise from that silent Ti, squaring in a weak way. Create fully in action, but do not cling to results. This is "arising from Ti to Yong."
  • Non-attachment: When action is complete, or when attachment arises, immediately perform the square root in a weak way. Do not claim merit, do not rely on ability, do not display self, do not exalt self. This is "returning Yong to Ti."
  • Four Weak Uses: Practice "non-contention yet naturally victorious" in conflict, "non-speaking yet naturally responsive" in speech, "non-summoning yet naturally coming" in waiting, "unhurried yet naturally planning" in planning.

4.4 Classic Verification

Using the "Ti-Xiang-Yong" architecture, ancient classics like Dao De Jing and Heart Sutra can be systematically re-read. Each chapter can be interpreted as an operation manual for "guarding Ti, seeing Xiang, skillful Yong."

Examples:

  • Chapter 11: "Being is for utility" (Yong), "Non-being is for use" (Ti). Being and non-being mutually produce — this is the law of Xiang.
  • Chapter 40: "Reversal is the movement of the Tao" (first law of Xiang), "The weak use of the Tao" (second law of Xiang). Reading both sentences together reveals the complete Xiang.
  • Chapter 65: "Do not govern with knowledge" (guarding Ti to govern Yong), "Always know the pattern, this is called mysterious virtue" (seeing Xiang to accord with Tao).
  • Chapter 72: "Know oneself without displaying self, love oneself without exalting self" (guarding Ti without clinging to Yong), thus able to "discard that, take this."
  • Chapter 73: "Non-contention, non-speaking, non-summoning, unhurried" — the four-fold expression of "the weak use of the Tao." "The Heavenly Net is vast, sparse yet not missing" — the ultimate revelation of Ti-Xiang non-duality.

Chapter 5: Scope of Application and Ethical Boundaries

This model applies to:

  • Interdisciplinary philosophical speculation and heuristic dialogue.
  • Theoretical support for body-mind cultivation, meditation, qigong, and other practice fields.
  • Attempts at modern translation of classical wisdom.
  • Deep reflection on personal life experience.

This model does not apply to:

  • Replacing modern medical diagnosis and treatment. For any physical symptoms, please first consult regular medical care.
  • As a rigorous theory of physics. Analogies in the model cannot be cited as basis for scientific papers.
  • Claiming absolute effectiveness of any cultivation method. The model provides only perspectives and operational frameworks, not promises.

This model insists on:

  • Acknowledging itself as a theoretical construction based on analogy and Ti-Yong philosophy, maintaining humility toward physics and classical originals.
  • Accepting rigorous criticism and calibration from any discipline, viewing it as the driving force for model evolution.
  • Continuously self-testing in practice, but not universalizing personal experience as absolute truth.
